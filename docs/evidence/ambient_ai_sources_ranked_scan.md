# SimMed ambient AI scribe evidence/source scan

Ranked sources for health-system simulation context. Evidence = peer-reviewed primary/review or formal HTA/policy source; Context = implementation, market, workflow, governance background not sufficient alone for model effect sizes.

1. NEJM AI — Ambient AI Scribes in Clinical Practice: A Randomized Trial — https://doi.org/10.1056/aioa2501000
Credibility: top-tier peer-reviewed AI/medicine journal; randomized design. Main claims: ambient scribes can be evaluated rigorously for documentation burden, quality, and clinician/patient outcomes. Limitations: setting/vendor/workflow specificity; RCT endpoints may not generalize to ROI across specialties. Use: EVIDENCE, high priority for effect-size assumptions.

2. JAMA — Changes in Clinician Time Expenditure and Visit Quantity With Adoption of AI-Powered Scribes — https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2026.2253
Credibility: top-tier peer-reviewed multisite study identified in PubMed. Main claims: evaluates actual time allocation and visit quantity after AI-scribe adoption. Limitations: observational adoption effects, potential confounding by enthusiastic early users and local implementation support. Use: EVIDENCE for productivity/time parameters, with sensitivity ranges.

3. JAMA Network Open — Clinician Experiences With Ambient Scribe Technology to Assist With Documentation Burden and Efficiency — https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.60637
Credibility: peer-reviewed, directly on ambient scribes. Main claims: clinician-reported effects on documentation burden and efficiency; useful for adoption and satisfaction assumptions. Limitations: experience/self-report outcomes less robust than objective time or cost; likely organization/vendor specific. Use: EVIDENCE for qualitative/adoption parameters; not sole proof of economic value.

4. NEJM AI — Does AI-Powered Clinical Documentation Enhance Clinician Efficiency? A Longitudinal Study — https://doi.org/10.1056/aioa2400659
Credibility: peer-reviewed longitudinal evaluation. Main claims: tracks efficiency over time, useful for ramp-up/learning curves. Limitations: nonrandomized; vendor and site implementation effects may dominate. Use: EVIDENCE for longitudinal adoption/learning assumptions.

5. Peterson Health Technology Institute (PHTI) — Administrative AI: Current Use and Potential Impact — https://phti.org/administrative-ai-current-use-and-potential-impact/
Credibility: independent health technology assessment organization; strong for value and implementation framing. Main claims: administrative AI may reduce burden/cost but value depends on workflow integration, validation, governance, and measurement. Limitations: broad administrative AI, not ambient scribes only; may synthesize early evidence rather than primary outcomes. Use: EVIDENCE/CONTEXT for value framework and model structure; not direct clinical effect size unless source is primary.

6. NEJM Catalyst — Ambient Artificial Intelligence Scribes to Alleviate the Burden of Clinical Documentation — https://doi.org/10.1056/cat.23.0404
Credibility: NEJM Catalyst implementation/case-study venue. Main claims: practical deployment lessons for reducing documentation burden. Limitations: case-study/management article, likely no randomized counterfactual; potential publication/vendor optimism. Use: CONTEXT for implementation pathways, governance, workflow; not primary evidence alone.

7. Annals of Internal Medicine / ACP — Rapid Evaluation of AI Technology Used for Ambient Dictation in Primary Care — https://doi.org/10.7326/ANNALS-25-02772
Credibility: ACP flagship peer-reviewed journal. Main claims: compares AI-generated and human-produced clinical notes; directly relevant to documentation quality and safety. Limitations: rapid evaluation scope; note quality may not equal downstream outcomes or cost savings. Use: EVIDENCE for quality/safety assumptions.

8. NPJ Digital Medicine — Barriers and opportunities of scaling ambient AI scribes across diverse healthcare settings — https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-026-02554-0
Credibility: peer-reviewed digital medicine journal. Main claims: identifies implementation barriers, equity, workflow, scaling factors. Limitations: may be qualitative/implementation-focused, not causal outcomes. Use: CONTEXT plus some EVIDENCE for implementation constraints.

9. JMIR Medical Informatics — Impact of an Ambient AI Scribe Among Clinicians and Patients: Real-World Prospective Observational Time-Motion Study — https://doi.org/10.2196/85580
Credibility: peer-reviewed prospective time-motion design. Main claims: objective workflow/time and patient/clinician impact in real-world setting. Limitations: observational, local confounding, limited generalizability. Use: EVIDENCE for time-flow parameters.

10. JAMIA — Clinicians’ rationale for editing ambient AI-drafted clinical notes — https://doi.org/10.1093/jamia/ocag059
Credibility: peer-reviewed informatics journal. Main claims: edits reveal persistent failure modes and areas needing human oversight. Limitations: does not by itself estimate productivity or ROI; may depend on specialty/vendor. Use: EVIDENCE for safety/quality-control burden and residual human review time.

11. Journal of Medical Systems — Validating Digital Scribes: A Scoping Review of Evaluation Practices and Clinical Use — https://doi.org/10.1007/s10916-026-02392-3
Credibility: peer-reviewed scoping review. Main claims: maps how digital scribes are evaluated and gaps in validation. Limitations: scoping review heterogeneity; does not produce pooled causal estimates. Use: EVIDENCE/CONTEXT for evidence-gap framing and metric selection.

12. AMA — Augmented intelligence / AI policy and physician resources — https://www.ama-assn.org/practice-management/digital/augmented-intelligence-health-care
Credibility: major US physician professional association. Main claims: physician-led AI, transparency, liability, privacy, validation, and workflow integration are central; AMA survey/workflow resources can inform adoption assumptions. Limitations: policy/advocacy and guidance, not primary evidence. Use: POLICY CONTEXT; not model evidence unless citing underlying studies.

13. WHO — Ethics and governance of artificial intelligence for health — https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240029200
Credibility: global public-health authority. Main claims: AI in health needs transparency, accountability, inclusiveness, human oversight, privacy, and safety. Limitations: broad AI governance, not ambient-scribe specific. Use: POLICY CONTEXT for guardrails and risk dimensions.

14. OECD — Recommendation of the Council on Artificial Intelligence / OECD AI Principles — https://legalinstruments.oecd.org/en/instruments/OECD-LEGAL-0449
Credibility: intergovernmental policy standard. Main claims: trustworthy AI should be human-centered, robust, transparent, accountable, and risk-managed. Limitations: not healthcare- or scribe-specific. Use: POLICY CONTEXT.

15. NHS England — AI and digital healthcare implementation guidance/AI Lab resources — https://transform.england.nhs.uk/ai-lab/
Credibility: national health-system implementation body. Main claims: safe deployment needs clinical safety, procurement, information governance, evaluation, monitoring. Limitations: UK regulatory/system context; not primary efficacy evidence. Use: POLICY/IMPLEMENTATION CONTEXT.

16. Stanford Medicine — ambient AI / clinical AI implementation news and case examples — https://med.stanford.edu/
Credibility: leading academic health system; useful implementation signal. Main claims: real-world pilots emphasize clinician burden reduction, patient communication, and governance. Limitations: institutional blog/news unless paired with peer-reviewed papers; risk of positive-publication bias. Use: CONTEXT only unless linked to primary studies.

17. Mayo Clinic — ambient documentation / generative AI clinical workflow case material — https://www.mayoclinic.org/ and https://www.mayoclinicplatform.org/
Credibility: leading health system/platform; practical implementation perspective. Main claims: ambient/genAI tools may improve documentation workflow with governance and validation. Limitations: blogs/vendor-partner material not independent evidence. Use: CONTEXT only unless backed by peer-reviewed evaluation.

18. ACP policy/Annals ecosystem — AI in clinical practice resources — https://www.acponline.org/clinical-information/clinical-resources-products/artificial-intelligence
Credibility: major internal medicine professional society. Main claims: emphasizes physician oversight, evidence, privacy/security, administrative burden reduction. Limitations: professional guidance, not effect-size evidence. Use: POLICY CONTEXT; Annals studies separately count as evidence.
