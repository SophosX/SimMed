Clinicians spend half their working day on electronic health records and then another hour or two at night. When it's done right, AI can help. More focus on patients, less burnout, and documentation that's still safe and accurate. That's the promise of Ambient Scribes. They are AI tools designed to work in the background, listen to clinical conversations, and automatically create transcripts, summaries, and medical notes. Hi, I'm Ellie Asgari, NHS kidney doctor and AI scientist at Newton's Street. In today's video, I'll explain how ambient AI technology works, why it needs careful evaluation, and how to deploy it safely in healthcare. So, let's begin. How exactly do ambient AIS work? Ambient Scribe technology uses speech recognition, natural language processing, and large language models, also known as LLMs, to transcribe and summarize conversations like clinical consultations. Unlike old dictation tools, these scribes are updated continuously and are trained based on context. They sometimes even suggest tasks. Next, we look at why testing is necessary. As the use of AI becomes common in healthcare, thorough testing becomes very important. Because LLMs keep updating, their outputs can change, which makes consistency a challenge, especially when patient safety is on the line. Mistakes from AI or over relying on it can cause errors in documentation or affect patient care. AI could also change how clinicians and patients interact. Regulatory guidance also highlights the need for safety, effectiveness, and responsible use. Let's look at some of the regulatory considerations. In the UK, the MH treats these tools as medical devices if they influence clinical decisions. That means they require certification, safety checks, and compliance with data protection laws like GDPR. In the US, the FDA is developing specific rules for generative AI in healthcare and HIPPO regulations ensure patient data privacy. In both cases, even tools not classified as medical devices need safety documentations. So, what are the metrics and methods available for evaluation? Well, before using these tools, it's important to check how well they transcribe speech, handle different accents or noisy settings, and avoid mistakes like adding or missing information. Key metrics include error rates, how much clinicians need to fix AI outputs, and agreement between humans and AI. After deployment, it is essential to monitor workflow impact, clinician satisfaction, fairness across patient groups, and track any unexpected issues. Finally, we have a list of recommendations to ensure the safe deployment of ambient scribes. These include one, doing data protection and safety assessments. Two, getting your clinical safety and data leads involved early. Three, knowing if your tool counts as a medical device. Four, planning thorough evaluations with human review of AI outputs. Five, training users to spot errors and understand limitations. And finally, number six, setting up ongoing monitoring and audits to catch any problems. Now, let's recap. In this video, you have learned how ambient AI has the potential to reduce clinician workload and improve care. But only with strong governance, monitoring, and following national guidelines. By following these recommendations, healthcare providers can reduce risk and increase the benefits AI can bring to both clinicians and patients.
