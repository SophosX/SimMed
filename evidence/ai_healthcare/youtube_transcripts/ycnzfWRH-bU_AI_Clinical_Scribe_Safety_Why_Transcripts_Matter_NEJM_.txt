Let's look at the summary of the current landscape as outlined by Goodman and Morgan. First, the issue of flattening. So AI scribes use large language models to summarize dialogue. Second is the problem of hallucinations. There are documented cases of AI scribes inventing diagnosis. For example, recording that a patient has diabetes or heart disease when neither was actually mentioned. If the transcripts deleted, it becomes nearly impossible to conduct a peer-reviewed audit of how these errors occur and whether clinicians are successfully catching them before they enter the permanent record. Third is the missed opportunity for clinical discovery. For the very first time in history, we have the technology to record clinical conversations at scale. This data could reveal how patients actually describe rare cancers years before diagnosis or whether diagnostic errors increase when clinicians interrupt patients more frequently or similar. By destroying this data, we're squandering a once- in a generation opportunity to study their mechanics of diagnosis and communication. We need independent evaluation of accuracy against the original transcripts. We need to know the error rates of these tools in the wild, not just in controlled vendor demonstrations and marketing materials. To overcome the liability hurdle, the authors propose three concrete actions. One, that clinicians and researchers must advocate for retention. Ultimately, new strategy protections similar to safe harbor laws may be required to shield transcripts from malpractice discovery while allowing their use for research and safety.
